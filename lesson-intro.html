Left to right: Neal Koblitz, Victor Miller, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman.


Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) was introduced independently by both Dr. Neil Koblitz at the University of Washington and Victor Miller working at I.B.M., each publishing their findings in different academic journals. Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman, working together, composed a way to exchange cryptographic keys in such a way as to prevent an evesdropper from gaining access to the encryption key. This became known as the Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange. Together, these two systems comprise a way for two people to share private information in a public forum such as the internet, without the need of exchanging a pre-shared secret key. Elliptic Curve Cryptography is used to generate keys, while the Diffie-Hellman key exchange is used to establish a shared private key.
